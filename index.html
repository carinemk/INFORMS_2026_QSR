Governance over Capacity: Detecting and Correcting the Consensus Trap in Distributed Credence Services

Carine Marette · Instructional Systems Technology, Indiana University
Abstract. We derive a closed-form validity ceiling for distributed evaluation under correlated rater error: adding raters drives inter-rater reliability toward one while benchmark concordance stays bounded below it. This yields the Consensus Trap, a process-monitoring diagnostic; on 1.98 million evaluations, governance restores process capability where capacity cannot.
Keywords: quality assurance; measurement validity; statistical process control; concordance; distributed evaluation.

1.Introduction

Distributed credence services (peer assessment, content moderation, clinical second-opinion panels, audit review) create value through many decentralized human judgments the recipient cannot verify even after consumption. The default quality strategy is capacity expansion: add evaluators to average out noise. This paper shows that capacity expansion fails under a measurable condition we call the Consensus Trap: high coordination reliability among evaluators masks low validity against an external benchmark, because evaluator errors are correlated rather than independent, so adding raters amplifies rather than cancels systematic error. We reframe the problem as one of quality-and-reliability assurance and show, on entire-population telemetry, that an agentic-AI governance capability restores statistical process capability more effectively than capacity expansion.

Hypotheses. We test five. (H1) Reliability can rise while validity falls, and under correlated error capacity expansion amplifies rather than corrects it. (H2) Governance improves operational performance more than capacity expansion. (H3) It does so by raising validity, not inter-evaluator reliability. (H4) The operations and marketing link is an association carried by engagement rather than by measured quality. (H5) Governance is a dynamic capability that improves over repeated cycles.

2.The Consensus Trap as a Quality-Assurance Failure Mode

Reliability is not validity. Reliability (L1) is inter-rater agreement (ordinal Krippendorff's α). Validity (L2) is concordance between the peer-aggregated grade and the instructor benchmark, by Lin's concordance correlation coefficient, which penalizes both correlation loss and mean/scale offset. The Consensus Trap is the conjunction

Trap ≡ (L1 ≥ 0.80) ∧ (L2 < 0.20),

confident, coordinated, and wrong; it emerges in 12.0% of framed activities. A non-compensatory override caps the quality score and halts release when it fires, so a high L1 can never offset a low L2.

A structural validity ceiling. Under the classical correction for attenuation, observed validity is bounded by reliability. When a component of error is shared, reliability still approaches one but validity is bounded below by the systematic share, a structural limit, not a convergence delay. The median validity ceiling is 0.58, with 60.4% of activities below 0.70; reaching adequate reliability by capacity alone would need a median of ~105 evaluators against a current median of 44.

A closed-form result. Let the peer mean estimate a submission's true score (variance σT2) through raters whose errors share a component of correlation ρ and variance σ2, plus a fixed bias b. As raters are added, reliability → 1, yet the peer mean's concordance with the benchmark is bounded above by

ρc★ = 2σT2 ÷ (2σT2 + ρσ2 + b2) < 1   (ρ > 0 or b ≠ 0).

Capacity cannot touch the shared-error term ρσ2 or the squared bias b2, so for most activities benchmark accuracy is unreachable by adding raters. This turns the diagnostic into a monitoring statistic.

3.The Monitor, and Why Agreement Charts Fail

The Consensus-Trap monitor reads both reliability and validity, estimates the ceiling, and flags and halts a trapped activity. The conventional monitor, a control chart on inter-rater agreement, is by contrast provably blind to the trap.

Algorithm 1   Consensus-Trap Monitor
  1. Input: peer marks {xij}, instructor anchor yi; thresholds τR, τV
  2. L1 ← 1 − mean range-ratio disagreement // reliability
  3. L2 ← normalized Lin's concordance of peer mean vs. anchor // validity
  4. estimate ceiling ρc★ from (L1, L2)
  5. if L1 ≥ τR and L2 < τV then flag Trap; cap the governance score; halt for human review
  6. else release
Proposition (Blindness of agreement monitoring). Under correlated error (ρ > 0), reliability → 1 for every value of the bias b, so any monitor that is a function of agreement alone assigns the trapped state the same limiting alarm probability as a healthy high-agreement state; its detection power cannot exceed its false-alarm rate (its ROC is the diagonal). Detecting the trap requires a benchmark-reading statistic, and by Neyman–Pearson the validity rule is the most powerful test of the trap under the model.

We confirm the theorem on data. Among 3,954 activities, 380 (9.6%) are materially biased against the expert (|bias| > 0.45). On the 190 confident-but-wrong activities (high agreement yet biased) the reliability chart detects 0% by construction, while the validity monitor detects 80.5%.

4.Governance as Statistical Process Control

Treating absolute grading bias as a quality characteristic against a material-bias tolerance of 0.45, governance read as statistical process control raises the process-capability index of grading error and roughly halves its variance (0.116 to 0.054).

5.Multi-Engine Measurement Validity

Peer marks are a per-criterion rubric value while the instructor anchor is a raw grade; comparing them directly collapses pooled concordance to 0.002. Normalizing each side within its own criterion before Lin's concordance restores validity to a median of 0.594. Validity is skill-specific: peers track the instructor least on abstract skills (Creativity 0.46, Critical Thinking 0.50) and most on concrete ones (Communication and Professionalism 0.55, Problem-Based Learning 0.56).

6.The Operations and Marketing Link is an Engagement Phenomenon

Deep-governance adopters churn less, an activity-count-controlled Cox model returns a protective hazard ratio, but this is not a governance-quality effect. A confounding decomposition is decisive: unadjusted, higher validity predicts higher churn, and once we condition on engagement (log activity count), validity and the composite governance score have no retention association while engagement stays strongly protective. A time-varying specification removes any immortal-time artifact and leaves the estimate unchanged; an E-value of 5.93 and Oster's δ* = 12.2 bound residual confounding. We report H4 as an engagement-linked association, not a quality-to-retention mechanism.

A latent-profile analysis of instructor governance layers (four profiles; N = 491) sharpens it: the best-retained profile has the lowest validity, while the highest-validity profile churns the most. Quality and retention decouple, which is the point: governance is a quality-and-reliability capability justified on its own terms, and a dynamic one, since cumulative prior governance lowers the hazard of an instructor's next Consensus-Trap event.

7.Conclusion

Distributed credence services possess a latent quality-and-reliability failure mode, the Consensus Trap, in which coordination reliability masks benchmark validity and capacity expansion is mathematically futile. An agentic-AI governance capability detects the trap with production-scale statistics and restores process capability where adding evaluators cannot. For quality managers, the result reframes "add inspectors" as futile whenever error is systematic and supplies a deployable alternative: monitor reliability and validity as separate control charts, fire the trap detector, and route a non-compensatory override to a human checkpoint. The same eight-layer stack and four engines drive the platform's live governance dashboard, making the diagnostic an operating instrument rather than a retrospective study.

Managerial implications. The results convert a governance intuition into a control procedure. Monitor reliability and validity on separate control charts; when the trap fires, do not add evaluators but apply the governance cascade (anchor to an expert exemplar, weight raters by demonstrated accuracy, route flagged cases to a human checkpoint); track the process capability of grading error and its variance as the operational key performance indicator, since capacity faces a structural ceiling that capability does not; and read validity per skill and per rubric criterion, coaching the low-validity skills the per-skill engine surfaces.

Boundary conditions. The mechanism applies wherever coordination reliability and benchmark validity can diverge and an external benchmark is intermittently observable; clinical second-opinion panels, content moderation, audit review, and professional certification share this structure. Where the benchmark is fully observable, governance reduces to ordinary quality control; where it is never observable, validity is unidentified and only reliability can be managed.
